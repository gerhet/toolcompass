How to Calculate Your Freelance Rate

Setting your hourly rate is one of the most challenging parts of being a freelancer. Many beginners simply look at what others are charging, but this is a mistake. Your rate should be based on your individual financial needs, your business overhead, and your billable capacity—not market averages alone.

⚠️ Financial Disclaimer: This calculator provides estimates for educational purposes only. Results are not financial or tax advice. Consult a licensed accountant or financial advisor for personalized guidance. ToolCompass is not responsible for financial decisions based on these calculations.

Key Factors to Consider

  • Billable vs. Non-Billable Hours: You won't spend 40 hours a week on client work. You need to account for marketing, admin, invoicing, and professional development.
  • The Cost of Being Human: Unlike employees, freelancers don't get paid for sick days or vacations. You must bake these "missing" hours into your rate to maintain income stability.
  • Taxes and Insurance: Depending on your location, you might need to set aside 25% to 40% of your income for taxes, health insurance, and retirement contributions.

When to Revisit Your Rate

Recalculate your hourly rate whenever your expenses change, you gain new skills, or market demand shifts. Many successful freelancers review their rates quarterly and increase them annually to account for experience and inflation.

Add your desired net income and annual business expenses. Divide this total by your annual billable hours (working weeks × hours per week). This gives your base rate. We recommend adding 30% for taxes and unexpected costs.

Billable hours are the hours you actually spend working for clients. As a freelancer, you cannot bill for admin, marketing, invoicing, or sick days, so your hourly rate must be higher to cover this non-billable time and ensure profitability.

The Safety Rate includes a recommended 30% buffer to cover income taxes, health insurance, retirement contributions, and unexpected business costs that aren't included in your monthly expense calculations. This helps ensure financial stability.
